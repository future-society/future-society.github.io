The 2028 Global Intelligence Dividend

A letter from June 2028, on the crisis that never came.

The boom that arrived instead of the collapse everyone positioned for, written as a retrospective against the same technological assumptions as the crisis scenario it mirrors.

Published February 2026 · Received June 2028 · 34 min read
Prologue
What follows is a scenario, not a forecast. It is written as a retrospective from June 2028, in the same spirit, and against the same technological assumptions, as the widely circulated “Global Intelligence Crisis” scenario. Every capability that breaks the economy in that letter exists in this one. The dates are invented, the companies are real, the numbers are illustrative, and the mechanism is the argument. Nothing here is investment advice.

By June 2028 the strangest fact about the American economy was how long everyone had waited for it to collapse.

The short positions had been elegant. The scenario pieces had been persuasive. Abundant machine intelligence would hollow out white-collar payrolls, the top decile of earners would stop spending, private credit would choke on software debt, and the whole edifice of annuities, mortgages, and the S&P would come down in the correct dramatic order. AI was exceeding expectations, and the market was AI. Only problem, the argument went: the economy was not.

The economists kept waiting for the demand hole to open. It never did, because every dollar that stopped flowing to friction started flowing somewhere else, usually within a quarter, usually to somebody with a higher propensity to spend it. The pessimists modeled the destruction with great precision and the redirection not at all. That asymmetry, more than any single technology, is what the last thirty months were about.

AI was exceeding expectations, and the market was AI. The surprise was that the economy became AI too, and the economy was bigger than the market.

How we got here happened in three phases. Each began exactly the way the crisis scenario said it would. Each ended somewhere else.

Phase I
Phase I · 2026

The Software Dividend

The first act is not disputed by anyone, bull or bear. In early 2026, AI coding agents made it feasible for a competent internal team to replace most of an enterprise SaaS contract in a quarter. Procurement teams noticed. Renewal negotiations at the large platforms turned into hostage exchanges, and seat-based pricing, the load-bearing wall of a $1.2 trillion industry, buckled. Enterprise software prices fell 40–60% in eighteen months. ServiceNow's infamous “build-it-ourselves” renewal season happened on schedule.

The crisis scenario ends the paragraph there: pricing power collapses, layoffs ripple through the vendor ecosystem, the doom loop engages. What actually happened next was less cinematic. The $310 billion a year that corporate America had been spending on software seats did not vanish. It sat, briefly, in CFO budgets, and CFOs in competitive industries cannot sit on windfalls, because their competitors don't. Within three quarters the savings had been marked to market as price cuts, capacity expansion, and hiring in every function that touched a customer. Software deflation turned out to be indistinguishable, at the macro level, from a corporate tax cut, except that it recurred every year and no one had to pass it.

The platforms themselves split into two species. Those that clung to seat pricing shrank with dignity. Those that switched to outcome pricing, pay per resolved ticket, per closed book, per shipped release, discovered that at one-fifth the price their addressable market was twenty times the size. The software industry's revenue fell 22% from its 2025 peak. The software economy, everything built, bought, and operated as code, roughly tripled, because 4.1 million businesses that could never have justified an enterprise contract were now running bespoke systems written by agents and supervised by a new job title, deployment engineering, that did not exist in 2025 and employed 900,000 people by the end of 2027. The median displaced SaaS professional was re-employed in 4.5 months at a 6% pay cut. Sharp and painful, but survivable.

Every dollar saved on friction flowed into demand that made the next round of hiring possible.

The loop, running in reverse
Phase II
Phase II · 2026–2027

The Great Fee Repeal

The second phase was the one nobody priced correctly, because it didn't show up in any company's earnings before it showed up in everyone's checking account.

Consumer agents killed friction-based business models exactly as advertised. Real estate commissions compressed from 2.7% to 0.9%. Travel booking take rates went to zero as agents assembled itineraries directly against airline and hotel APIs. Wealth management fees fell from 100 basis points to fifteen. Agents routed a growing share of commerce over stablecoin rails, and interchange, the 2% toll on essentially all American consumption, began its long repricing. DoorDash's moat (“you're hungry, this app is on your screen”) evaporated on schedule.

The crisis scenario read this as revenue destruction, and it was. But a fee is a transfer, not value, and the destruction of a transfer has a recipient. Add up the intermediation costs that agents repealed between mid-2026 and the end of 2027 and you get roughly $740 billion a year returned to households: the largest tax cut in American history, passed by no Congress, signed by no president, effective immediately and forever.

For the median household this was worth about $4,100 a year, invisible as a line item, unmistakable as a balance. And because it accrued disproportionately to the bottom eight deciles (who pay interchange, commissions, and distribution fees on nearly everything, and own almost none of the companies that collected them), it landed on the highest marginal propensity to consume in the economy. The top 10% of earners drive half of discretionary spending; the pessimists were right about that. What they missed is that a broad-based windfall doesn't need the top decile. Restaurant traffic, domestic travel, home improvement, youth sports, live events: the entire “someone does something for you” economy inflected upward through 2027 while the price of everything transacted through a screen fell.

The intermediaries themselves were repriced, not destroyed. The card networks kept the one thing agents couldn't replicate, a trust and dispute layer with sixty years of legal scaffolding, and sold it at a quarter of the old toll on three times the volume. Machines optimizing for price did not care about your favorite app. It turned out consumers didn't care either. They cared about the $4,100.

Phase III
Phase III · 2027

The Great Rebalancing

Here is where the two scenarios genuinely diverge, so it is worth being precise about what the crisis version predicted: a 2% decline in white-collar employment producing a 3–4% hit to discretionary spending, displaced knowledge workers flooding into lower-wage sectors and compressing them, a spiral with no natural brake.

The displacement happened. Routine white-collar employment, coordination, reporting, first-drafting, the great middle of the org chart fell 8% between January 2026 and mid-2028, roughly 1.9 million positions. Anyone who tells you this was painless is selling something. What did not happen was the spiral, for three reasons the doom models weighted at zero.

First, the vacancies were already there. The United States entered 2026 with 1.1 million unfilled healthcare positions, 600,000 open roles in the skilled trades, and an electrical grid buildout driven, with some irony, by AI datacenter demand, that needed every electrician the country could certify. The crisis scenario treated displaced workers as surplus. The labor market treated them as inventory.

Second, AI collapsed the cost of becoming someone else. The same models that displaced the analysts retrained them. An AI preceptor took a career-switcher to journeyman electrician in fourteen months instead of four years; nursing programs built around individualized AI instruction graduated cohorts in under two. The Occupational Mobility Compact of March 2027 (forty-one states fast-tracking licensure reciprocity) was the single highest-ROI piece of legislation of the decade, and it cost almost nothing. Companies caught on the wrong side of the disruption borrowed to fund their way across it: “transformation facilities” became the fastest-growing category in private credit, with lenders underwriting the cost base a borrower would have after the transition rather than the revenue line it had before.

Third, and this is the one that broke the models, the wage structure inverted. Compensation compressed at the top of the org chart and exploded at the bottom of the hourly market, where all that fee-repeal demand was landing on a limited supply of humans. Median nominal wages grew 6.2% in 2027 against 0.9% inflation: the largest real median wage gain since 1998. The consumption base broadened in exactly the years the pessimists said it would narrow. A $180,000 strategy VP becoming a $95,000 infrastructure project lead is a tragedy in one spreadsheet and a rounding error in the national accounts, because three people earning $52,000 became three people earning $71,000 over the same interval, and they spent nearly all of it.

Deflation is only a crisis when incomes fall faster than prices. Incomes won the race by four lengths.

Flywheel

The Flywheel: How Finance Amplified It

Private credit, the dog that didn't default

Zendesk's $5 billion ARR-backed facility, the largest of its kind ever written, and Exhibit A in every crisis deck, was supposed to become the largest software default in private credit history when autonomous customer service ate its book. Instead it became the template. The company repriced to resolution-based billing, tripled its customer count among businesses that had never paid for support software, and refinanced in March 2027 at 140 basis points inside the original spread. Direct lending default rates for 2027 came in at 1.1%, a record low, because lenders discovered that a borrower whose costs are collapsing faster than its prices is, whatever its revenue line says, improving as a credit.

The annuity machine finds its asset

The alternative managers (Apollo, KKR, Blackstone) had spent a decade funneling life-insurance annuity deposits into private credit, and the crisis scenario cast this as the systemic fault line. It turned out to be the transmission mechanism. Permanent capital is exactly the wrong funding for a panic and exactly the right funding for a buildout, and 2026–28 was the largest buildout in American history: $2.1 trillion of datacenters, grid, robotics, and modular housing, matched against liabilities that don't run. The “permanent capital” narrative was supposed to be tested by a wave of redemptions. It was tested instead by a wave of demand, which is a nicer exam.

Mortgages: the crisis that required falling incomes

The $13 trillion residential mortgage market assumes stable incomes for thirty years, and the bears were right that no one had stress-tested it for white-collar obsolescence. No one got to run the test. Serious delinquencies ended 2027 at 47 basis points, the lowest ever recorded, because the marginal mortgage holder is not a strategy VP; she is a nurse married to an electrician, and both of them just got the two largest raises of their lives. Meanwhile AI-driven permitting and factory-built construction added 2.9 million units in three years; house prices drifted 4% lower while ownership rose two full points. The mortgages were sound the day they were signed, and the world only improved afterward.

The macro release valve

Assemble the pieces: productivity compounding near 5%, unit labor costs falling, CPI at 0.8%. The Fed, staring at the first supply-driven disinflation since the 1990s, cut to 1.75% into an expanding economy. It was no rescue: holding real rates at 4% against a productivity boom is how you cause the recession everyone had already written up. Cheap capital met visible demand. Margins expanded, wages rose, and prices fell all at the same time, which every textbook says is picking three from a menu of two. Productivity paid for the third.

The Policy Dividend

Congress, for once, legislated ahead of the loop instead of behind it, largely because the crisis scenario was so widely read that hedging it became bipartisan. Two acts mattered:

  • The Abundance Dividend Act of 2027 placed a 3% levy on frontier inference compute and paid it straight back out: a $150 monthly dividend to every adult, plus wage insurance covering 80% of any compensation gap for twenty-four months after an AI-attributed displacement. Total cost, a rounding error against the receipts windfall; total effect, the removal of the fear that had been suppressing consumption more than any actual job loss.
  • The American Compute Reserve of 2027 took warrant positions in frontier labs in exchange for fast-tracked federal land, power, and water: a sovereign wealth fund assembled not by taxing the boom but by owning a slice of it. First distributions are scheduled for 2030. Norway, but for intelligence.

Note what was not needed: no emergency QE, no bailout of private credit, no mortgage forbearance regime. The tools of 2008 and 2020 stayed in the drawer. When incomes rise faster than prices, most policy problems turn out to be optional.

Positioning

Positioning: What Worked

We are not making recommendations; we are describing a scenario's winners with the same license the crisis piece used to describe its casualties.

  • AI infrastructure held up through both scenarios; NVDA and TSM are the picks and shovels, and that is what picks and shovels do. The surprise was everything downstream of the electron: grid equipment, transformers, electrical contractors, utilities with datacenter exposure.
  • The buildout complex ran hot: homebuilders and modular construction, robotics integrators, industrial REITs, permanent capital's new permanent assets.
  • Anything charging a human premium: airlines, live events, hospitality, restaurant groups, the whole experience economy, levered to the broadest real income gain in fifty years.
  • Main Street finance came back into fashion: regional banks rediscovered as lenders to four million new micro-businesses, with staffing, credentialing, and trades-training platforms as the tollbooths of the Great Rebalancing.
  • The repriced rather than the destroyed: payments networks, outcome-priced software, insurance distribution, bought in the panic of the crisis narrative at single-digit multiples by whoever read the fee repeal as volume rather than death.

The casualty list is short and undramatic: pure intermediation with no trust function, seat-priced software that refused to reprice, and commercial office, which, to be fair, was already a casualty.

The jobs were good on day one. The world just got better after the offer letters were signed.

Epilogue

Epilogue: February 2026

Now the uncomfortable part, which you already suspected: you are not reading this in June 2028. You are reading it in February 2026, and none of the loops described above have engaged. The SaaS repricing is just beginning. The fee repeal is a few procurement memos and a stablecoin pilot. The Occupational Mobility Compact does not exist. Neither, yet, does the spiral.

Here is what should bother you. This scenario and the crisis scenario share every assumption about the technology: the same coding agents, the same consumer agents, the same collapse of friction, the same displacement of the org chart's middle. They run on identical inputs. They differ on exactly one variable, what people do with a windfall, and on that variable, the historical record is not ambiguous. Windfalls get spent. Vacancies get filled. Prices that fall while incomes rise are called, in every previous instance we can find, a boom.

The bearish scenario requires the $740 billion to disappear. This one only requires it to move. We know which assumption needs the footnote.

Position for the dividend, not the crisis. Or at the very least, hedge your pessimism. It has, historically, been the expensive tail.
